Overview

Four years of monthly gatherings, 8,150+ people, 29 events — and a platform designed to get lighter as the archive grows heavier.

29 events, 40 speakers, 60 startups — each connected to the others, not listed in isolation. The platform surfaces relationships, not just records.
A living archive — every gathering adds to a growing, searchable record that reflects the community's trajectory over time, not just its latest moment.
One entry, fully managed — a non-technical team runs the entire cadence. No developer needed. No duplication. No drift between what happened and what's published.

Challenge

A growing community, a growing maintenance burden

Every month brought a new event, new flyers, new speaker bios, new startup pitches — and no structure tying them together. The operational weight was starting to outpace the community's ability to document itself.

Approach

Model the community, not the content

Designed a relational CMS before a single pixel: events, speakers, and startups as interconnected collections. The website became a view of the data — not a container for it. Content structure first, visuals second.

Outcome

An archive that grows itself

One CMS entry per month populates five surfaces. Homepage counters, archive grid, event detail pages, speaker profiles, and startup listings update automatically — with zero duplication, zero syncing, zero code.

Design

Editorial, not ornamental

Typography and spacing carry the hierarchy — the community's character lives in the details, not the decoration.

Minimal palette, restrained motion, editorial serif. A site built to age well across hundreds of events, not peak in a single season.

Key Feature

One entry.
Every surface.

Adding a monthly event is a single CMS action. Behind it, the platform fans out automatically — the homepage refreshes, counters increment, archive cards generate, speaker profiles update, and startup listings extend. The non-technical team never touches a template.

CMS Architecture

Four collections.
One community.

The schema mirrors how the community actually works — events hold the context, speakers and startups carry their own histories, and the flyer archive gives the collection a visual memory. Every relationship is a multi-reference, not a copy.

Events

Central collection — date, flyer, registration link, multi-references to speakers and startups

Speakers

Profile collection — name, photo, bio, LinkedIn; one speaker, many appearances

Startups

Pitch collection — founder, LinkedIn, referenced by the event they pitched at

Flyers

Visual archive — image-led collection, paginated by design, auto-populates the archive page

Relational schema

A website shaped like the community — not like a content list.

Living Archive

Every gathering, preserved.

Each monthly flyer joins a paginated CMS archive — an image-led record of the community's trajectory. Every tile deep-links to its event, speakers, and startups, turning the visual history into a navigable one.
